02 — Problem Space

The Gap Between Data and Action

Modern consumers are drowning in health data from wearables and annual blood panels — but almost none of it translates into meaningful daily action. Healthcare remains fundamentally reactive.

How might we help health-conscious adults turn fragmented biometric data into a clear, daily understanding of their body — so they can prevent illness rather than treat it?

Core User Problems

🗂️

Data Fragmentation

Sleep data lives in one app, steps in another, blood work in a PDF. There's no single source of truth for a person's health.

🤯

Interpretation Overload

Raw biomarker numbers (HRV, hsCRP, fasting glucose) are meaningless without clinical context. Users don't know if 68ms HRV is good or bad.

⏳

Reactive Healthcare

Doctors only see patients when something is wrong. Between visits, users have no guidance — trends deteriorate silently for months.

📉

Motivation Drop-off

Existing health apps are either too clinical (intimidating) or too gamified (shallow). Neither creates lasting behaviour change.

Market Context

The Opportunity

  • Global digital health market projected at $1.5T by 2030
  • Over 30% of adults now use a wearable device daily
  • Preventive care reduces long-term healthcare costs by up to 40%
  • AI health assistants growing at 45% CAGR

Competitive Gap

  • Apple Health aggregates but doesn't interpret data
  • Oura/Whoop track sleep/recovery but lack lab integration
  • Function Health offers labs but no daily biometric layer
  • No product unifies wearables + labs + AI coaching end-to-end

04 — Design Strategy

Principles & North Star

"Don't show users more data — show them less, smarter. Every number should earn its place."

1

Clarity Over Completeness

Surface only what matters right now. Progressive disclosure hides complexity until the user opts in.

2

Context Drives Action

Every metric must be paired with an interpretation and a recommendation, not just a number.

3

Trust Through Transparency

Cite sources, show trends not just snapshots, explain the "why" behind every AI insight.

4

Daily Ritual Design

Build for a 60-second morning check-in habit. Speed and glanceability over depth-first flows.

5

Earned Gamification

Streaks and scores only where they reinforce genuine behaviour change, never as a distraction.

6

Dark Mode as Default

Health data consumption often happens at waking or bedtime. Dark UI protects circadian rhythm and feels premium.

06 — Design System

Visual Language

Philosophy

PRVNT's visual language draws from medical precision and consumer premium — the legibility of clinical tools with the warmth of a personal coach. The dark theme is non-negotiable: users check health data at dawn and bedtime, and blue light suppression is a product value, not just an aesthetic choice.

Every colour in the palette carries semantic meaning and is used consistently across the entire app. Score colours (green / amber / red) mirror clinical standards. Metric accent colours are assigned once and never reused for different metrics.

07 — Screen Design Decisions

Screen-by-Screen Rationale

🏠

Home — The Daily Check-in

Goal: Complete picture of today's health status in under 60 seconds. The user opens the app each morning and knows immediately what their body needs.

Key Design Decisions

  • Triple activity rings (Recovery / Sleep / Readiness) above the fold — mirrors Apple Watch familiarity, immediately scannable
  • Today's Plan transforms data into action — tasks are driven by biometric state, not generic advice
  • Sleep card uses a single-colour progress bar (not a rainbow chart) — keeps the card focused and uncluttered
  • HRV chart includes day labels and average reference line — a bare line with no axis context is meaningless
  • Notification panel as a drop-down overlay — keeps the header clean without a separate screen

Card Order Rationale

1

Activity Rings

Highest-level status summary — Recovery, Sleep, Readiness

2

Key Metrics Row

HRV, Resting HR, SpO₂ — the 3 most clinically significant daily signals

3

Today's Plan

Turns scores into actions immediately while motivation is high

4

Steps + Sleep + HRV

Detailed cards for users who want to go deeper

5

Habits + Coming Up

Longer-term tracking for committed users

📊

Vitals — Deep Biometric Dive

Goal: Let users understand each metric deeply — not just the current value, but trends, context, and what to do about it.

Key Design Decisions

  • Expandable VitalCards use progressive disclosure — compact by default, full detail on tap. Reduces visual overload for 8 metrics
  • Heart rate chart has 3 time windows (7D/14D/30D) with a dashed average reference line — context anchors the data
  • Sleep stages redesigned 3 times. Final: compact 4-column segmented timeline bar + 4-chip legend. Not bars (already on Home), not a donut (too complex for this screen)
  • Per-metric AI insight inside expanded card — positive/neutral/caution sentiment drives colour, headline, and tip
  • Score rings animate on open (not on scroll) to reinforce the interaction reward

Reports — Lab Intelligence

Goal: Make lab results and health records feel accessible and actionable — not intimidating PDFs from a clinic portal.

Key Design Decisions

  • Overall Health Score hero at the top — users need a single number before they drill down into system-level detail
  • Body Systems grid directly below the score — it's the breakdown of that score, the logical visual flow
  • Lab Results accordion hides marker detail by default — a Comprehensive Metabolic Panel has 12+ markers; showing them all at once is overwhelming
  • 6-Month Biomarker Trends uses tabbed AreaChart (HDL / LDL / Glucose) — trends matter more than snapshots for preventive health
  • Upload FAB replaces inline button — uploading is a primary, frequent action; a floating pill above the nav bar is always reachable without scrolling
  • No gradient on Health Score card — gradients added visual noise. Plain surface + border matches the rest of the app's card language

08 — UX Decision Log

Why We Made Each Call

A selection of the most significant design decisions, the alternatives considered, and the reasoning behind the final choice.

Decision Alternatives Considered Rationale
Dark mode as default Light mode, system-adaptive Users check health data at dawn and bedtime. Dark UI protects circadian rhythm. Also signals "premium medical" not "fitness tracker".
Bottom nav (5 tabs) Hamburger menu, tab bar + FAB, sidebar Thumb reach on mobile. All 5 destinations are equally primary — no hierarchy justifies hiding any of them.
Activity rings over bars Bar chart, score tiles, text summary Rings communicate completeness toward a goal intuitively. Familiar from Apple Watch. Three rings (Recovery/Sleep/Readiness) side by side = immediate trivariate status.
Expandable VitalCards Separate detail screen, always-expanded list, tabs Reduces cognitive load — 8 metrics expanded is overwhelming. Tap to expand is a clear affordance. Smooth height animation rewards interaction.
Upload as FAB Header button, inline banner, bottom of page Upload is a high-frequency primary action. FAB is always reachable without scrolling, springs in on load, clearly communicates "do something here".
Health Snapshot → Score tiles Body silhouette SVG, radar chart, organ heatmap Body silhouette read as "robot" by test users. Radar chart was visually complex and poorly understood. Score tiles are scannable, coloured by status, no chart literacy required.
Sleep card: single bar + 2 stats Donut chart, column bars, segmented bar + legend User feedback: "cramped", "too much". The key sleep facts are duration and score. One progress bar (toward 8h goal) + Sleep Score + Deep Sleep is the minimal complete picture.
Daily Challenge: SVG art Emoji icon, photo placeholder, gradient blob An illustrated scene (moonlit forest) creates genuine emotional resonance and uniqueness vs every other health app using emoji in a coloured circle.
09 — Interaction Design

Motion & Micro-interactions

Every animation in PRVNT serves a functional purpose — confirming state, revealing hierarchy, or creating a sense of data "arriving" from a live source.

Animation Principles

  • Spring physics for all interactive elements (whileTap, FAB entry, sheet open) — feels physical, not mechanical
  • Staggered entrances on grids and lists — items appear 60–80ms apart, communicating a collection of individual items
  • Score ring draw on mount with cubic-bezier [0.16, 1, 0.3, 1] — overshoot easing feels satisfying and "earned"
  • Height animation on expandable cards via Framer Motion layout — avoids the choppy clip effect of CSS max-height
  • AnimatePresence mode="wait" on chart tabs — clean cross-fade prevents two charts overlapping mid-transition

Key Micro-interactions

  • Task check-off — scale bounce [0.7 → 1.2 → 1] on the checkbox, strikethrough + opacity fade on text
  • Habit toggle — icon swaps to checkmark, border colour fills with habit colour, all in 200ms
  • Score rings — animated strokeDashoffset draw from 0 to value over 0.9s
  • Progress bars — width animates from 0 to target, staggered by index in lists
  • FAB spring — scale: 0 → 1 with spring stiffness 380, damping 22 on screen mount
  • Notification dot — pulsing scale + opacity loop to attract attention without being aggressive
⚡

Performance constraint: All charts use Recharts with YAxis hide width={0} — not YAxis hide. The difference: hide alone still reserves SVG width, causing number overflow into the card edge. This bug affected every chart before being identified and fixed globally.

10 — Design Iterations

What Changed & Why

Documenting the real iteration history — including the ideas that didn't work — is as important as showcasing the final designs.

Iteration 01 — Health Snapshot

Human body silhouette SVG

Built an anatomical SVG with organ hotspots (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys) pulsing as motion.circle elements. Users described it as "looks like a robot" and "clinical in a bad way". The interaction model — hovering organs to see scores — had no mobile-native equivalent.

RemovedReplaced with Health Score Card + Body Systems grid
Iteration 02 — Body Systems

Horizontal bar chart for 6 body system scores

Used a bar chart to compare cardiovascular, metabolic, hormonal, etc. Feedback: "too many bars everywhere — Vitals already has bars, the step card has bars". Replaced with a 3×2 score tile grid — each tile shows emoji, large score, status badge. Visually distinct, more scannable.

Removed barsScore tiles with emoji + gradient background
Iteration 03 — 6-Month Trends Chart

Single-line plain AreaChart with no context

"Looks really bad like just a line drawn." The chart had no tabs, no current value, no delta indicator. Rebuilt with: 3-tab selector (HDL/LDL/Glucose), large current value hero, improving/declining delta badge, average reference line, and a bottom stat row showing Dec vs May vs Δ.

Bare line chartTabbed chart with full context layer
Iteration 04 — Sleep Stages (Vitals)

Flat segmented bar + dot legend

The flat 24px segmented bar with a 2-column dot legend was described as "looks really bad". Redesign attempt 1: 2×2 tiles (too big). Redesign attempt 2: column bars (user already had bar fatigue). Final: thin 8px segmented bar + 4 compact chips showing stage, duration, percentage. Matches Vitals screen language without adding bulk.

Flat bar v12×2 tiles (too big)Column barsThin bar + 4 chips
Iteration 05 — Daily Challenge Card

Yellow/gold gradient with emoji icon

Original used #FFD60A gold palette with a 🧘 emoji in a coloured box. Feedback: "colors look bad", "why an icon instead put something like a picture". Replaced emoji with a custom SVG illustration — moonlit forest scene with layered hills, pine trees, crescent moon, and a walking figure. Palette shifted to mint green (#34D399) for a fresh, outdoorsy feel. XP removed as it felt hollow.

Gold gradient + emojiSVG forest illustration + mint green
Iteration 06 — Home Sleep Card

Rainbow segmented bar → donut chart → simplified

Sleep card went through 3 visualisations: (1) rainbow segmented bar + dot legend — "cramped", (2) SVG donut with 2×2 legend — "lot going on", (3) Final: single progress bar toward 8h goal + Sleep Score chip + Deep Sleep chip with emoji on right. Clean single-colour approach removes the visual clutter of 4 stage colours.

11 — AI Strategy

The Intelligence Layer

PRVNT's AI isn't a chatbot bolted on — it's woven into every screen as a contextual interpretation engine, with a dedicated coach interface for deeper conversations.

AI Touch Points

Per-Metric Insight (Vitals)

Each expandable VitalCard generates a sentiment-aware headline, body copy, and tip based on the metric's value, trend direction, and status. Three sentiment tiers: positive (green), neutral (amber), caution (red).

Today's Insight (Home)

Priority-ranked insight card pulled from the insights data layer. Surfaces the single most important pattern detected — e.g. "Recovery dropped 12% this week, HRV trending lower since Wednesday."

AI Coach Chat (Dedicated screen)

Full conversational interface. Opens with a context-aware morning greeting referencing the user's actual recovery score and HRV. Knows the user's biometric history, suggesting workout adjustments in real time.

Lab Result Translation (Reports)

Biomarker values are automatically classified as Optimal / Fair / Needs Attention with reference range bars showing where the user sits relative to clinical norms — not just high/low flags.

AI Coach Sample Interaction

🤖

Good morning, Alex! Your recovery score is 78% today — slightly below your 7-day average of 83%. I noticed your HRV dropped to 68ms after Tuesday's high-intensity session.

I feel a bit tired. My recovery score was low this morning.

🤖

That makes sense. Your HRV also dropped slightly — likely from the Zone 4 workout yesterday. I'd recommend keeping today's activity to Zone 1–2 max. A 20-minute walk and some light stretching would be ideal.

13 — Key Learnings

What This Project Taught

On Health UX Specifically

  • Data without context creates anxiety, not empowerment. Every number needs a "so what?"
  • Users have extremely low tolerance for visual clutter in health apps — they're already stressed when they open them
  • Colour has stronger semantic weight in health than anywhere else — red means danger, green means safe. Don't fight this convention
  • Dark mode is a health product decision, not just an aesthetic one — think about when and where the app is used
  • Biometric charts need axes and reference lines — a bare line chart is meaningless without context anchors

On Product Iteration

  • The Health Snapshot went through 3 failed iterations before finding the right answer — sometimes the right solution is to remove the concept entirely
  • "Looks bad" feedback is directional, not prescriptive — it means "this doesn't fit the mental model", not "change the colour"
  • Page ordering is a UX decision as much as component design — the same cards in different order tell completely different stories
  • Progressive disclosure (expandable cards) is more valuable in health apps than in almost any other category
  • Illustrations > emoji when emotional resonance matters — the SVG forest scene for the Daily Challenge creates genuine character

The best health app isn't the one that shows you the most data. It's the one that makes you feel capable of acting on it.
